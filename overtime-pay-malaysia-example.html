Overtime salary guide

Overtime Pay Malaysia Example

Overtime pay is a crucial component of your total income if you work beyond your normal working hours. Under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955, eligible employees are entitled to overtime compensation at prescribed rates. This page explains how overtime pay is calculated, provides a worked example using a RM3,000 salary, and highlights important things to watch out for.

How Overtime Pay Works Under the Malaysia Employment Act

The Malaysia Employment Act 1955 governs overtime pay for employees who are covered under the Act. This includes:

  • Manual labour employees: Regardless of salary level
  • Non-manual employees: Earning RM4,000 or less per month (as per current threshold)

Normal working hours are capped at 8 hours per day or 48 hours per week. Any work performed beyond these hours qualifies as overtime and must be compensated at the prescribed rates.

Overtime Rate Formulas

The overtime pay rate depends on the type of day on which the overtime is performed:

Day TypeHours WorkedOvertime Rate
Normal working dayIn excess of normal hours1.5× hourly rate
Normal working day (after 2 OT hours)3rd hour onwards2× hourly rate
Rest day (half day or less)Up to half normal hours½ day's wages
Rest day (more than half day)More than half, up to normal hoursFull day's wages
Rest day (overtime)In excess of normal hours on rest day2× hourly rate

Deriving the Hourly Rate from Monthly Salary

The hourly rate is a critical figure for overtime calculations. Under the Employment Act, it is derived as follows:

Hourly Rate = Monthly Salary ÷ 26 ÷ 8

The number 26 represents the statutory number of working days in a month (used for calculating daily and hourly rates under the Act), and 8 is the normal number of working hours per day.

For a RM3,000 monthly salary:

  • Daily rate = RM3,000 ÷ 26 = RM115.38
  • Hourly rate = RM115.38 ÷ 8 = RM14.42

EPF, SOCSO, and PCB Implications on Overtime Pay

Overtime earnings are treated as wages and are subject to all standard deductions:

  • EPF: Both employee (11%) and employer (12% or 13%) contributions apply to overtime pay. Your overtime earnings accumulate in your EPF account alongside your regular salary contributions.
  • SOCSO: The 0.5% employee contribution applies to overtime wages.
  • EIS: The 0.2% employee contribution applies to overtime wages.
  • PCB: Overtime pay increases your total monthly income for PCB purposes. Using the PCB 1M method, LHDN recalculates your annual tax liability including overtime, and the marginal tax on the additional income is deducted in that month. For lower-income earners, this may not result in additional PCB if total annual income remains below the taxable threshold.

Common Mistakes When Dealing With Overtime Pay

  • Not checking your employment contract: Some contracts may specify different overtime terms or flat OT allowances. Always check your contract against the Employment Act requirements to ensure you are receiving the correct entitlement.
  • Assuming all OT is paid at the same rate: Overtime on rest days and public holidays has different (often higher) rates compared to normal working days. Make sure you know which rate applies.
  • Not keeping track of OT hours: Maintain your own record of overtime hours worked and cross-check against your payslip. Discrepancies are common and should be raised with HR promptly.
  • Forgetting that OT is subject to deductions: Your gross OT pay is not what you take home. After EPF, SOCSO, EIS, and potentially PCB, the net amount is lower.
  • Exceeding the monthly OT cap: The Employment Act limits overtime to 104 hours per month. Working beyond this may expose your employer to legal penalties and could affect your health and safety.
  • Assuming all employees qualify for OT: Employees earning above RM4,000 per month in non-manual roles, certain managerial positions, and some exempt categories may not be covered under the Act's overtime provisions. Check your employment category.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the overtime pay rate under Malaysia Employment Act?

Under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955, overtime pay rates depend on the type of day. For a normal working day, overtime is paid at 1.5 times the hourly rate for the first 2 hours and 2 times the hourly rate for subsequent hours. On rest days, the rate ranges from 1.5x to 2x depending on hours worked. On public holidays, overtime is paid at a minimum of 3 times the hourly rate.

How is the hourly rate derived from a monthly salary in Malaysia?

The hourly rate is calculated by dividing your monthly salary by 26 (the statutory number of working days in a month under the Employment Act) and then dividing by 8 (the normal daily working hours). For example, a RM3,000 monthly salary gives an hourly rate of RM3,000 ÷ 26 ÷ 8 = RM14.42 per hour. This is the base rate used for all overtime calculations.

Is overtime pay subject to EPF and SOCSO contributions?

Yes. Overtime pay is considered part of your wages under Malaysian labour law and is subject to EPF, SOCSO, EIS, and PCB deductions. Your employer must deduct the standard employee EPF rate (11%), SOCSO (0.5%), and EIS (0.2%) from your overtime earnings. Overtime pay also adds to your cumulative income for PCB calculation.

Can my employer refuse to pay overtime?

If you are covered under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955 (earning RM4,000 or less per month for non-manual workers, or any salary for manual workers), your employer is legally required to pay overtime for work exceeding the normal working hours, subject to a cap of 104 overtime hours per month. If your employer refuses, you can lodge a complaint with the Labour Department (Jabatan Tenaga Kerja) under the Ministry of Human Resources.

Does overtime on a rest day or public holiday pay more?

Yes. The Employment Act specifies higher rates for rest days and public holidays. On a rest day, if you work less than half your normal hours, you receive half a day's wage. If you work more than half but not exceeding normal hours, you receive a full day's wage. Beyond normal hours, overtime is 2x the hourly rate. On public holidays, you receive at least 3x the hourly rate for all overtime hours worked.

Is there a limit to how many overtime hours I can work in Malaysia?

Yes. Under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955, the maximum overtime allowed is 104 hours per calendar month. Employers who require employees to exceed this limit are in violation of labour law. However, some exceptions may apply under specific circumstances. Employees earning above RM4,000 per month in non-manual roles may have different terms depending on their employment contract.
