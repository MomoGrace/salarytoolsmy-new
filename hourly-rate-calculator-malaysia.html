Hourly wage guide

Hourly Rate Calculator Malaysia

Knowing your hourly rate is useful for many purposes — calculating overtime pay, comparing job offers with different pay structures, evaluating freelance opportunities and understanding the true value of your time. In Malaysia, hourly rates are typically derived from monthly salary figures, and understanding the correct formula and assumptions is essential to get an accurate result.

How Hourly Rate Is Derived from Monthly Salary

Most Malaysian employees receive a fixed monthly salary rather than an explicit hourly rate. To determine the hourly equivalent, a standard formula is applied:

Hourly Rate = Monthly Salary ÷ Working Days Per Month ÷ Working Hours Per Day

The most commonly used assumptions in Malaysia are:

  • Working days per month: 26 days (this is the standard assumption in the Employment Act 1955 for salary proration and overtime calculations)
  • Working hours per day: 8 hours (the normal hours of work under the First Schedule of the Employment Act)

Why 26 Working Days?

The 26-day divisor is a convention that averages out the working days across a full year, accounting for rest days (typically Sundays). In a year with 52 weeks, there are 52 rest days, leaving 313 working days (365 – 52). Dividing by 12 months gives approximately 26.08 days per month, which is rounded to 26 for payroll simplicity.

Some organisations, particularly in the tech and corporate sectors where employees work Monday to Friday only, use 22 working days per month instead. This yields a higher daily and hourly rate but only applies if the company's policy specifies this method.

Worked Example: RM4,000 Monthly Salary

Let's calculate the hourly rate for an employee earning RM4,000 per month using the 26-day method:

Step Calculation Result
1. Monthly salary Given RM4,000.00
2. Daily rate RM4,000 ÷ 26 days RM153.85 per day
3. Hourly rate RM153.85 ÷ 8 hours RM19.23 per hour

If the same salary were calculated using 22 working days (Monday to Friday only):

Step Calculation Result
1. Monthly salary Given RM4,000.00
2. Daily rate RM4,000 ÷ 22 days RM181.82 per day
3. Hourly rate RM181.82 ÷ 8 hours RM22.73 per hour

The difference of approximately RM3.50 per hour between the two methods highlights why it is important to know which convention your employer uses.

When Hourly Rate Matters

Understanding your hourly rate is essential in several practical scenarios:

Overtime Pay Calculation

Under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955, overtime rates are multiples of your ordinary hourly rate:

  • Normal working day overtime: 1.5x hourly rate for the first two extra hours, 2x for subsequent hours.
  • Rest day overtime: 2x hourly rate (up to half day's work), 2x for the second half (full day).
  • Public holiday overtime: 3x hourly rate.

For example, if your hourly rate is RM19.23 and you work 3 hours of overtime on a normal working day, your overtime pay would be: 2 hours × RM19.23 × 1.5 = RM57.69 plus 1 hour × RM19.23 × 2 = RM38.46, totalling RM96.15.

Part-Time Work Comparison

If you are considering a part-time role, understanding the hourly equivalent of your current full-time salary helps you evaluate whether the part-time offer is financially competitive. A part-time job paying RM20 per hour may sound attractive, but when you factor in the loss of EPF employer contributions, paid annual leave and medical benefits, it may be less favourable than it appears.

Freelance Rate Comparison

Freelancers in Malaysia often quote project fees based on hourly or daily rates. To compare a freelance offer against your full-time employment:

  • Calculate your full-time hourly rate using the formula above.
  • Add a 20–30% premium to account for self-employed EPF contributions, unpaid leave, medical expenses, and professional development costs.
  • For a RM19.23/hour full-time rate, a competitive freelance rate would be approximately RM23–RM25 per hour.

Unpaid Leave and Salary Proration

When you take unpaid leave, your employer will deduct the daily rate from your salary. Using the correct hourly and daily rates ensures the deduction is accurate. For example, taking 2 days of unpaid leave on a RM4,000 salary (26-day method) means a deduction of 2 × RM153.85 = RM307.70.

Malaysia Employment Act and Minimum Hourly Considerations

The Employment Act 1955 specifies a minimum monthly wage rather than a direct minimum hourly rate. As of recent revisions:

  • The minimum monthly wage for Peninsular Malaysia is RM1,500.
  • This translates to approximately RM1,500 ÷ 26 ÷ 8 = RM7.21 per hour using the 26-day method.
  • Employees in Sabah, Sarawak and Labuan may have different minimum wage rates.

The Employment Act also defines the maximum normal working hours as 8 hours per day or 48 hours per week (averaged over a period of up to 3 weeks). Any work beyond these limits qualifies for overtime pay.

Common Mistakes in Hourly Rate Estimation

  • Dividing by 30 or 31 calendar days: This includes rest days and produces an artificially low hourly rate that understates your actual earning rate per working hour.
  • Using gross salary without considering deductions: While the hourly rate for overtime purposes is based on gross salary, your effective earning rate after EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB deductions is lower. Be clear about which figure you need.
  • Not checking the company's specific method: If your employer uses 22 days instead of 26, quoting a rate based on the wrong assumption will lead to incorrect calculations for overtime or leave deductions.
  • Ignoring public holidays and annual leave: In practice, you do not work 26 days every month. Public holidays and approved leave reduce actual working days, but the 26-day convention already accounts for this on average.
  • Confusing gross hourly rate with net hourly rate: Your gross hourly rate (before deductions) is what matters for overtime and employment law. Your net hourly rate (after deductions) is what matters for personal budgeting.
  • Forgetting overtime premiums: When comparing hourly earnings across jobs, remember that overtime is typically paid at 1.5x to 3x the ordinary rate. A lower base hourly rate with frequent overtime may out-earn a higher base rate with no overtime.

Frequently Asked Questions

What formula is used to derive an hourly rate from a monthly salary in Malaysia?

The most widely used formula is: Hourly Rate = Monthly Salary ÷ 26 working days ÷ 8 working hours per day. For example, RM4,000 ÷ 26 ÷ 8 = RM19.23 per hour. Some organisations use 22 working days (excluding weekends) instead of 26, which would give RM22.73 per hour. Check your employment contract or company policy to determine which method applies to you.

Is the 26 working days assumption standard across all Malaysian companies?

No. While 26 working days is a common convention used in many payroll systems in Malaysia, it is not legally mandated. Some companies use 22 working days (Monday to Friday only), some use actual calendar days minus rest days, and others may follow the specific formula outlined in the Employment Act for overtime purposes. The correct divisor depends on your employment contract, industry practice and HR policy.

When is knowing my hourly rate important for overtime calculations?

Your hourly rate is essential for calculating overtime pay under the Malaysia Employment Act 1955. Overtime rates are typically 1.5x the ordinary hourly rate for the first two extra hours on a working day, 2x for subsequent hours, and 2x for work on rest days. For public holidays, the rate is usually 3x. Without knowing your correct hourly rate, you cannot verify whether your overtime pay is calculated accurately.

Does the Malaysia Employment Act define a minimum hourly wage?

Malaysia's Employment Act specifies a minimum monthly and daily wage rather than a fixed hourly rate. The minimum monthly wage for Peninsular Malaysia is RM1,500. However, for the purpose of overtime and other calculations, an hourly rate can be derived by dividing the monthly minimum wage by working days and hours. This effectively sets a floor for hourly earnings for covered employees.

How do I compare a freelance hourly rate against a full-time monthly salary?

When comparing freelance and full-time income, remember that freelancers do not receive EPF employer contributions, paid leave, medical benefits or job security. To make a fair comparison, calculate your effective full-time hourly rate by dividing your monthly take-home pay (after deductions) by actual working hours. Then add an estimated 20-30% premium to the freelance rate to account for self-employed EPF contributions, leave days, medical expenses and income tax that freelancers must manage independently.

What mistakes should I avoid when estimating my hourly rate from salary?

Common mistakes include using gross salary instead of net take-home pay, dividing by 30 or 31 calendar days instead of working days, forgetting that different months have different numbers of working days, not accounting for public holidays when estimating actual billable hours, and using the wrong working hours per day if your company has a different standard. Always verify your assumptions against your employment contract.
