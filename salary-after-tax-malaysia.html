After-tax salary guide

Salary After Tax Malaysia

Salary after tax (also called take-home pay or net salary) is the amount of money you actually receive in your bank account each month after all mandatory deductions have been subtracted from your gross salary. In Malaysia, understanding your after-tax salary is essential for budgeting, loan applications, and financial planning.

What Does Salary After Tax Mean in Malaysia?

In Malaysia, your employer is legally required to deduct several items from your gross monthly salary before paying you. These deductions typically include:

  • EPF (KWSP) — Employee Provident Fund: 11% of your gross salary (or a reduced rate if you are aged 55–60 or 60 and above)
  • SOCSO (PERKESO): 0.5% of your monthly wage for the Employment Injury Scheme
  • EIS (SIP): 0.2% of your monthly wage for the Employment Insurance System
  • PCB (Potongan Cukai Bulanan): Monthly tax deduction as an advance payment towards your annual income tax, calculated using LHDN's prescribed schedules

The remainder after subtracting all these deductions is your salary after tax.

How PCB Monthly Deduction Relates to Annual Income Tax

PCB is not a separate tax — it is simply a mechanism where your employer withholds a portion of your salary each month and pays it directly to LHDN on your behalf as an advance on your annual income tax. Think of it like monthly instalments towards your total tax bill for the year.

At the end of the financial year, you must file your tax return (Form BE for individuals with employment income). LHDN then compares your actual tax liability against the total PCB already deducted. If you overpaid, you receive a refund. If you underpaid, you must pay the balance. This is why your monthly PCB may not perfectly match your final tax liability.

How EPF Relief Reduces Your Taxable Income

One of the most important reliefs for Malaysian employees is the EPF tax relief. Under Section 44(11A) of the Income Tax Act 1967, your employee EPF contribution (up to RM4,000 per year for employees below age 55) is deducted from your gross income before tax is calculated. This effectively lowers the income bracket your salary falls into.

For example, if you earn RM5,000 per month and contribute 11% to EPF, your annual employee EPF contribution is RM6,600. However, only RM4,000 qualifies for the relief. So your chargeable annual income for tax purposes is reduced by RM4,000 compared to a scenario with no EPF relief.

Common Mistakes When Estimating After-Tax Salary

  • Forgetting EPF relief: Some people apply the tax rate to their full gross salary without deducting eligible EPF relief, leading to an overestimate of their tax.
  • Not including allowances: Fixed allowances (transport, housing, etc.) are often taxable and should be added to gross income before calculating deductions.
  • Assuming flat tax rate: Malaysia uses progressive tax rates (0% on the first RM5,000, then 1%, 3%, 8%, 13%, 21%, 24%, and up to 30%). Applying a single rate to your entire salary is incorrect.
  • Ignoring SOCSO and EIS: These small deductions add up and affect your net pay.
  • Not filing tax returns: If your PCB was over-deducted and you do not file a return, you miss out on potential refunds from LHDN.

Malaysia Payroll Notes

LHDN publishes an updated PCB Schedule (Kaedar PCB/CP38) annually. Employers use this schedule to determine the exact monthly tax deduction for each employee based on their monthly taxable income, number of children, and spouse relief status. If your personal circumstances change (marriage, number of children, additional reliefs), submit an updated copy of your latest tax return or TP1 form to your HR department so LHDN can revise your PCB schedule accordingly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is salary after tax calculated in Malaysia?

Salary after tax in Malaysia is calculated by subtracting PCB (Potongan Cukai Bulanan) income tax, EPF employee contribution, SOCSO and EIS deductions from your gross monthly salary. The remaining amount is your take-home pay. PCB deductions are based on LHDN's monthly tax deduction schedules and depend on your monthly taxable income after EPF relief.

Does EPF contribution reduce my taxable income?

Yes. In Malaysia, your employee EPF contribution (up to RM4,000 per year, or RM7,000 for EPF members aged 55 and above as of 2024) qualifies as a tax relief under Section 44(11A) of the Income Tax Act 1967. This means a portion of your gross salary is shielded from PCB and income tax, effectively lowering your monthly tax deduction.

Is PCB the same as my final income tax liability?

No. PCB is a monthly deduction made by your employer as an advance payment towards your annual income tax. After the financial year ends, you must file your income tax return with LHDN (e-BE/e-B form). If your total PCB deducted is more than your actual tax, you receive a refund. If it is less, you must pay the balance.

What common mistakes do people make when estimating after-tax salary?

Common mistakes include: forgetting to account for EPF relief which lowers taxable income, not including allowances in gross pay, assuming the tax rate applies to the full salary rather than progressive tax brackets, ignoring SOCSO and EIS deductions, and not filing a tax return to claim refunds for over-deducted PCB.

What is the tax rate for a RM5,000 monthly salary in Malaysia?

A RM5,000 monthly salary gives an annual gross of RM60,000. After EPF relief (RM6,600 at 11%), the chargeable income is approximately RM53,400. Under LHDN progressive tax rates, the first RM5,000 is tax-free, the next RM15,000 at 1%, and the rest at progressively higher rates. The estimated annual tax is around RM1,700 to RM2,000, which translates to roughly RM140 to RM170 in PCB per month.

How can I reduce my monthly PCB deduction?

You can lower your PCB by ensuring your employer submits an updated LHDN tax deduction schedule that reflects all eligible reliefs (EPF, medical expenses, life insurance, education, etc.). Submit a copy of your tax return or TP1 form to your HR department so LHDN can recalculate your monthly PCB based on accurate relief claims.
