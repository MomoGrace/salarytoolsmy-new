How to File Income Tax in Malaysia: Step-by-Step Guide

Quick summary: If you are filing income tax for the first time (or filing again after a break), this guide walks you through what to prepare, how to file through LHDN e-Filing, how to reduce common mistakes, and how to use SalaryToolsMY calculators for planning. Rules may change, so always check the latest LHDN and other official government sources before submitting.

Who this guide is for

This guide is for Malaysian employees, fresh graduates entering the workforce, part-time workers with employment income, and anyone who receives an EA form and needs a practical filing process. It is also useful if you changed jobs, received bonus or allowance payments, or noticed your PCB deductions differ from your expected annual tax.

If you are handling complex business income, cross-border tax issues, or unusual relief claims, this guide can still help with workflow—but you should verify details with official guidance or a qualified tax professional.

Before you start: understand the filing objective

Your annual filing is a reconciliation exercise. During the year, your employer may deduct PCB from salary. During filing season, you submit actual annual information: total employment income, applicable reliefs, and other relevant details. LHDN then determines whether your final tax position is balanced, overpaid, or underpaid.

The goal is not to “guess the right number quickly.” The goal is to submit a complete and supportable return using consistent records. If you approach filing this way, stress drops significantly.

Step-by-step: how to file income tax in Malaysia

Step 1: Confirm your tax profile and filing period

Check that your personal details are current in your filing profile. This includes identity details, contact info, and residency-related context that may affect filing treatment. If there is any mismatch between your payroll records and profile, resolve it first.

Step 2: Review your EA Form before data entry

Do not copy numbers blindly. Compare EA entries with your year-end payroll summary and at least a sample of monthly payslips. If you changed employers within the year, keep all employment records clearly separated, then combine only where required in the return.

Step 3: Prepare your relief worksheet first

Many mistakes happen because people claim reliefs while rushing through form fields. Instead, prepare a simple worksheet before logging into filing:

  • List relief category name.
  • List your supporting receipts/documents.
  • Record your conservative claim amount based on evidence.
  • Mark uncertain categories for final official verification.

Step 4: Enter income and deduction information carefully

Use your EA form as the anchor, then cross-check with payslips for anomalies such as bonus months or unpaid leave periods. If your annual values look unusually high or low compared with your gross salary pattern, pause and re-check before submission.

Step 5: Enter reliefs with evidence-based discipline

Claim only what you can support. If a receipt is unclear, duplicated, or outside the relevant period, treat it cautiously. A smaller clean claim is better than a larger unsupported claim that creates future issues.

Step 6: Reconcile against PCB deducted during the year

Compare your computed annual outcome with total PCB deducted. Differences are normal, especially after bonuses, job changes, or relief updates. The point is to understand why the difference exists, not to force a match.

Step 7: Final quality check before submit

  • Identity and profile details correct.
  • Income entries tied to EA and payroll records.
  • Reliefs backed by receipts.
  • No obvious transposition errors (extra zeroes, decimal mistakes).
  • Save draft and review once more after a short break.

Malaysia-specific examples

Example A: Employee with fixed salary and no bonus

Amir receives stable monthly salary with standard deductions. His filing is straightforward: EA form review, limited relief categories, quick reconciliation against PCB. His main risk is overclaiming relief due to poor receipt organization.

Example B: Employee with bonus in one quarter

Siti receives a large bonus that changes her annual tax position. Her PCB in bonus months appears high, but that does not automatically mean overpayment. She compares annual totals, then checks whether relief claims reduce final payable amount.

Example C: Employee changed jobs mid-year

Daniel changed employers in July. He maintains separate records from both employers and combines information correctly during annual filing. His biggest risk is missing one employment record and underreporting income.

Submission-day checklist

  • All mandatory fields completed.
  • EA and payroll entries reconciled.
  • Relief claims documented.
  • Draft reviewed once after a break.
  • Submission proof saved (PDF/screenshot/acknowledgement number).
  • Reminder created to keep records for future reference.

After submission: what to do next

Archive your final submission copy and all supporting evidence in one folder labeled by assessment year. If you receive a notice or need to clarify an entry, organized records will save time and stress. Continue monthly payroll checks so next year’s filing is easier.

Most importantly, keep cautious with assumptions: official interpretations, forms, and treatment can change. Always check the latest official LHDN and relevant government guidance before making financial decisions.

Detailed filing timeline (recommended)

4–6 weeks before filing: gather EA form, payroll summaries, and receipts; create a relief tracker. 2–3 weeks before filing: pre-calculate a conservative estimate and flag uncertain categories. 1 week before filing: perform a full draft run-through. Submission day: submit only after final review and archive proof immediately.

This timeline helps you avoid deadline stress and prevents simple mistakes such as category mix-ups, missing documentation, or incomplete entries. If your payroll history includes bonus months, resign-and-join transitions, or unpaid leave periods, add extra review time.

Extra caution points for first-time filers

First-time filers often focus on “finish fast” instead of “finish correctly.” Slow down on the fields that matter most: annual income totals, relief amounts, and profile details. If any field is unclear, pause and check official guidance before continuing.

Another common issue is mixing personal finance assumptions with tax filing obligations. Budget plans are useful, but filing should be evidence-led. Keep your filing record separate from your monthly budgeting sheet.

Extended practical guidance

In real life, financial planning is rarely static for a full year. Employees and employers deal with salary revisions, one-off allowances, bonus payments, leave adjustments, and changing household needs. A strong guide should therefore emphasize process quality, documentation discipline, and consistent monthly review. Instead of waiting until year-end, make a short monthly review habit: compare expected and actual figures, note unusual differences, and log any unresolved questions. This habit prevents compounding errors and supports better decisions during filing season or payroll audits.

For Malaysian readers, another useful practice is scenario planning. Build three planning cases: conservative, typical, and stretch. The conservative case assumes higher essential spending and lower discretionary room. The typical case reflects your normal month. The stretch case assumes positive events like bonus or stronger project income but still keeps risk controls in place. Scenario planning is valuable because it prevents overconfidence and helps you maintain emergency readiness. When used with calculator-based estimation, this method improves visibility without creating false certainty.

Communication quality also matters. If you are an employee, ask concise questions with document references rather than broad assumptions. If you are an employer or payroll team member, provide clear payslip labels and consistent policy explanations. Most payroll or tax confusion is not caused by advanced technical complexity; it is caused by incomplete context, rushed deadlines, and missing records. A shared language around “source document,” “assumption,” and “verification step” significantly reduces friction.

Finally, remember that rules, system flows, and acceptable documentation standards can evolve. A guide like this is best used as a structured framework, not as a legal guarantee. Keep the framework, refresh the details. Before final decisions, always verify the latest official EPF, SOCSO, LHDN, PERKESO, and relevant government references. If your case is unusual, escalate early to qualified professionals. This approach balances speed, compliance, and confidence.

Final pre-submission self-audit

Before final submission, run a self-audit: verify identity details, review year totals against payroll trends, and ensure every relief has supporting evidence. If an item is uncertain, treat it cautiously and verify through official channels. This final audit step is often the difference between a smooth filing experience and a stressful correction cycle.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these rules guaranteed to match the latest government update?

No. Rules and rates can change. Always check the latest official EPF, SOCSO, LHDN or government source before making financial decisions.

Should I rely only on online calculators?

Use calculators for planning and education, then verify with official portals, payroll records, and professional advice where needed.

What is the difference between gross and net salary?

Gross is before deductions; net is what you receive after statutory and other deductions. This difference affects budgeting and tax planning.

How often should I review my payroll details?

At least monthly for payslips and quarterly for bigger trends, then a full review before year-end tax filing or major life changes.

Can these guides replace professional advice?

No. They are educational guides. Seek licensed tax, legal, or payroll professionals for case-specific decisions.

Where can I start if I am confused?

Start with your latest payslip, then use SalaryToolsMY calculators for estimates, and compare against official statements or HR records.
