PCB tax guide

PCB Calculation Example Malaysia

PCB (Potongan Cukai Bulanan), also known as Monthly Tax Deduction (MTD), is the mechanism used by Malaysian employers to deduct income tax from employees' salaries every month. Instead of paying a large tax bill at the end of the year, PCB spreads your estimated annual tax liability across 12 monthly deductions. This page walks through how PCB is calculated, provides a worked example and explains the common pitfalls that employees encounter.

How PCB Is Derived from Taxable Income

The PCB calculation follows a specific sequence defined by LHDN (Inland Revenue Board of Malaysia). Understanding each step helps you verify the tax deduction on your payslip:

  1. Step 1 – Determine gross monthly salary: Start with your total monthly remuneration including basic salary, fixed allowances and any regular commissions.
  2. Step 2 – Subtract EPF employee contribution: Deduct your EPF contribution (default 11% of gross salary) from the gross amount. This reduced figure is your taxable monthly income for PCB purposes.
  3. Step 3 – Identify MTD Schedule category: Select the correct category from LHDN's MTD Schedule based on your marital status, whether your spouse works and the number of children you are claiming.
  4. Step 4 – Look up PCB amount: Find the row in the schedule table that matches your taxable income range. The figure in that row is your monthly PCB deduction.
  5. Step 5 – Employer withholds and remits: Your employer deducts this amount from your salary and remits it to LHDN on your behalf by the 10th of the following month.

Key Concept: EPF Reduces Taxable Income for PCB

One of the most important rules in PCB calculation is that the EPF employee contribution is deducted from gross salary before tax is calculated. This is provided under Section 44(11A) of the Income Tax Act 1967. The effect is significant:

  • Without EPF deduction: PCB is calculated on full gross salary (higher tax).
  • With EPF deduction: PCB is calculated on a lower taxable income (lower tax).

This means that electing a lower EPF contribution rate to increase take-home pay also has the side effect of increasing your PCB, because your taxable income becomes higher. There is a trade-off between immediate take-home pay and tax savings.

Worked Example: PCB for RM5,000 Monthly Salary

Consider an employee with the following profile:

  • Monthly gross salary: RM5,000
  • Marital status: Single, no children
  • EPF election: Default rate of 11%
  • No additional allowances or tax reliefs

Calculation Steps

Step Description Amount
1 Gross monthly salary RM5,000.00
2 EPF employee contribution (11%) – RM550.00
3 Taxable monthly income (for PCB) RM4,450.00
4 MTD Schedule category Category 1 (Single, 0 children)
5 PCB amount (from LHDN schedule) ~RM215.00

The exact PCB amount depends on the current year's LHDN MTD Schedule. The figure above is an approximation for illustration. Always refer to the latest published schedule on hasil.gov.my.

If the Same Employee Were Married with 2 Children

With the same RM5,000 salary, a married employee with 2 children and a non-working spouse would fall under a different MTD Schedule category (Category 3 — married with 2 children). Because this category offers higher personal relief, the monthly PCB for the same taxable income of RM4,450 would be significantly lower — potentially zero or close to zero for lower income ranges. This illustrates why two employees with identical salaries can have very different PCB deductions.

The PCB Schedule Concept (LHDN Tables)

LHDN publishes the MTD Schedule (often referred to as the PCB Schedule or Kaedah Potongan Cukai Bulanan) annually. The schedule contains multiple categories covering different employee profiles:

Category Description
Category 1 Single, no children
Category 2 Married, one non-working spouse, no children
Category 3 Married, one non-working spouse, 1 child
Category 4 Married, one non-working spouse, 2 children
Category 5 Married, one non-working spouse, 3 children
Categories 6–16 Additional categories for more children and different spouse working statuses

Each category table maps ranges of taxable monthly income to a fixed PCB amount. Your employer simply looks up your taxable income in the correct table and deducts the corresponding amount. The system is designed to be straightforward for payroll processing.

Why PCB Can Differ Between Employees at the Same Salary

Two colleagues earning RM5,000 each may see different PCB amounts on their payslips for several reasons:

  • Marital status and dependants: A married employee with children qualifies for higher reliefs, resulting in lower PCB.
  • Spouse employment status: If the spouse is not working, additional relief applies under certain categories.
  • EPF election rate: An employee who elected a lower EPF rate (e.g., 8% instead of 11%) has higher taxable income and therefore higher PCB.
  • Zakat deduction: Muslim employees who pay zakat have this deducted before PCB is calculated.
  • Mid-year joining: An employee who started work partway through the year may be on a different PCB calculation method (annualised).
  • Additional taxable allowances: Housing allowance, travel allowance or other benefits can increase taxable income even if basic salary is the same.

How Bonuses and Commissions Affect PCB

When you receive a bonus, commission or other additional income, your employer must calculate an additional PCB for that month. The LHDN method for bonus PCB works as follows:

  1. Annualise the additional income: Multiply the bonus by (12 minus the number of months already worked in the year) to estimate the annual additional income.
  2. Add to annualised salary: Combine with your annualised regular salary.
  3. Calculate annual PCB: Determine total annual PCB on the combined figure.
  4. Subtract regular PCB already paid: The difference becomes the bonus PCB deduction for that month.

This method often results in a noticeably larger deduction in the bonus month. It is important to plan for this when expecting bonus payouts so you are not caught off guard by a lower-than-usual take-home amount.

Common PCB Mistakes

  • Treating PCB as the final tax: PCB is an advance payment. Your actual tax is determined when you file your BE form. You may receive a refund or owe more.
  • Not updating HR after life changes: Getting married, having a child or your spouse starting work can change your MTD Schedule category. If you do not inform HR, you may be over-deducted or under-deducted.
  • Ignoring the EPF-PCB relationship: Reducing EPF to increase take-home pay will increase PCB, partially offsetting the benefit.
  • Assuming zero PCB means zero tax: Even if your monthly PCB is zero due to low income or high reliefs, you still need to file an annual tax return if you are a tax resident.
  • Forgetting about bonus PCB: Budget for a higher deduction in months when you receive a bonus or commission.
  • Not checking payslip PCB against the schedule: Errors in category selection or data entry can lead to incorrect deductions over many months.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the step-by-step process to calculate PCB from a monthly salary?

First, determine gross monthly salary. Second, subtract the EPF employee contribution (typically 11%) to get taxable income. Third, identify the applicable LHDN MTD Schedule category based on marital status and number of children. Fourth, look up the taxable income in the correct schedule row to find the monthly PCB amount. The resulting figure is the deduction your employer withholds each month.

How does EPF employee contribution reduce the taxable income for PCB purposes?

Under Section 44(11A) of the Income Tax Act 1967, the EPF employee contribution is allowed as a deduction from gross salary before calculating PCB. For example, if your gross salary is RM5,000 and your EPF contribution is RM550 (11%), your taxable income for PCB purposes is RM4,450. This reduces your monthly tax deduction, but electing a lower EPF rate means less deduction and higher PCB.

What are the LHDN MTD Schedule categories and how are they used?

LHDN publishes the MTD (Monthly Tax Deduction) Schedule with categories based on the employee's personal circumstances. Categories include single with no children, married with one non-working spouse and no children, married with one working spouse, and various categories for married employees with 1 to 20 children. Each category has its own table that maps taxable income ranges to a fixed monthly PCB amount.

Why might two employees with the same monthly salary have different PCB amounts?

Several factors can cause PCB to differ between employees at the same salary level: marital status (single vs married), number of children claimed, whether the spouse is working or not, different EPF election rates, additional tax reliefs or deductions, zakat paid by Muslim employees, and whether the employee joined mid-year. Each factor changes the MTD Schedule category or the taxable income used for PCB lookup.

Does receiving a bonus or commission affect the PCB calculation?

Yes. Bonuses and commissions are added to your gross salary for the month they are paid, which increases your taxable income. LHDN provides a separate PCB calculation method for additional income (bonus PCB), where the additional amount is annualised, PCB is calculated on the combined annual income, and then the regular monthly PCB is subtracted to determine the additional PCB for that month. This often results in a larger deduction in bonus months.

What should I do if I think my PCB deduction is incorrect?

First, check which MTD Schedule category your employer is using by reviewing your payslip or asking HR. Verify your marital status and number of children are correctly registered. Confirm your EPF contribution rate. If the issue persists, contact the LHDN branch handling your employer's file or submit an inquiry through the LHDN portal. Any excess PCB deducted will be refunded when you file your annual tax return, while underpayment will need to be settled.
