Annual income guide

Annual Salary Calculator Malaysia

Understanding your annual salary, including bonuses and supplementary income, is an important part of financial planning in Malaysia. Your annual income is not just your monthly salary multiplied by 12 — bonuses, allowances, and other payments all contribute to your total earnings for the year and affect your income tax calculation.

What Is Bonus and Supplementary Income in Malaysia?

In Malaysia, bonus and supplementary income refers to any payment made to an employee that is outside their normal monthly salary. This includes:

  • Annual bonus: Typically 1 to 3 months' salary, paid at the employer's discretion
  • Performance bonus: Linked to KPIs or company performance
  • Festival bonus (wang hari raya / wang gaji): Common in certain industries
  • Commission payments: Sales or project-based commissions
  • Fixed allowances: Housing, transport, and entertainment allowances (if not exempt)

All of these are generally considered taxable income by LHDN and must be included when calculating your annual income tax.

How Is Bonus Taxed Differently From Monthly Salary?

Malaysia uses the PCB 1M method (Potongan Cukai Bulanan) to calculate tax on bonus and supplementary income. Here is how it works conceptually:

  1. Step 1: Calculate your total cumulative annual income (all monthly salaries received so far + current month salary + bonus)
  2. Step 2: Calculate the total annual tax on this combined amount using LHDN's progressive tax rates
  3. Step 3: Subtract all PCB that has already been deducted in previous months
  4. Step 4: The remaining amount is the PCB to deduct from the bonus payment

This means the bonus is effectively taxed at your marginal tax rate — the highest rate that applies to your top portion of income. If a large bonus pushes you into a higher bracket, the additional tax can be significant.

Bonus and EPF/SOCSO Treatment

Bonus payments in Malaysia are subject to the same statutory contributions as regular salary:

  • Employee EPF: 11% of the bonus is deducted (or a lower statutory rate for those aged 55 and above)
  • Employer EPF: 12% or 13% of the bonus (depending on employee's EPF wage bracket), paid by the employer on top
  • SOCSO: 0.5% of the bonus for the Employment Injury Scheme
  • EIS: 0.2% of the bonus for the Employment Insurance System

These deductions further reduce the net bonus you receive. For a RM10,000 bonus, you can expect roughly RM1,100 in employee EPF, RM50 in SOCSO, and RM20 in EIS to be deducted, plus the additional PCB.

Common Mistakes When Calculating Bonus Tax

  • Assuming bonus equals full take-home: Many employees are surprised when their net bonus is significantly lower than the gross amount. Always factor in EPF, SOCSO, EIS, and PCB deductions.
  • Not accounting for bracket creep: A large bonus can push your total annual income into a higher tax bracket, increasing the marginal tax rate applied to the bonus portion.
  • Forgetting that EPF relief has a cap: The EPF tax relief is limited to RM4,000 per year (or RM7,000 for those aged 55+). Once your annual EPF contributions exceed this, additional EPF does not provide further tax relief.
  • Ignoring allowances as taxable income: Fixed allowances paid alongside your bonus may also be taxable and can affect your total PCB.
  • Not updating LHDN records: If you have additional reliefs (medical, education, life insurance), submit your TP1 form or latest tax return to HR so your bonus PCB is calculated more accurately.

Malaysia-Specific Notes on Bonus Tax Calculation

LHDN's PCB 1M calculation is performed by your employer's payroll system using the official tax deduction schedule. Employers who use the LHDN e-Data PCB system will have the bonus PCB automatically computed. If you receive multiple bonuses in a year, each subsequent bonus is added to the cumulative income, and the marginal tax effect compounds. Year-end filing (Form BE) reconciles the total PCB deducted against your actual tax liability, so any overpayment is refunded.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is bonus income taxed in Malaysia?

In Malaysia, bonus and supplementary income are taxed under the PCB 1M (Potongan Cukai Bulanan) system. LHDN treats your bonus as part of your annual income and calculates tax on the combined total using the progressive tax rates. This means your bonus is often taxed at your marginal rate, which can result in a larger deduction than expected compared to your regular monthly salary.

Will my take-home bonus be lower than the stated amount?

Yes, typically. Your employer will deduct EPF (11%), SOCSO, EIS, and PCB from your bonus before paying you. Because the PCB on bonus income can be substantial — especially if the combined annual income pushes you into a higher tax bracket — your net bonus may be significantly less than the gross figure. Use a bonus tax calculator to estimate the deductions.

Does my employer contribute EPF on my bonus as well?

Yes. Both the employee and employer must contribute EPF on bonus payments. The employee contribution is 11% (or a reduced rate for those aged 55 and above) deducted from the bonus. The employer contributes 12% or 13% depending on the employee's EPF wage ceiling, and this is paid by the employer on top of the bonus.

What is the difference between PCB on salary and PCB on bonus?

PCB on salary is calculated monthly based on the LHDN tax deduction schedule using your monthly taxable income. PCB on bonus uses the PCB 1M method, which adds the bonus to your cumulative annual income and recalculates the total tax for the year using progressive rates. The difference between this new total tax and the PCB already deducted in prior months becomes the PCB for the bonus month.

Can I receive my bonus without PCB deduction?

It depends. If your employer uses the LHDN e-Data PCB system and your cumulative annual income after the bonus is still below the taxable threshold (RM35,000 for unmarried employees or RM50,000 with spouse relief), no PCB may be deducted from the bonus. However, EPF, SOCSO, and EIS are still mandatory. Some employers may also apply a flat percentage or their own estimation method.

How should I plan financially when expecting a bonus in Malaysia?

Expect to receive roughly 65% to 80% of your gross bonus as take-home pay, depending on your income bracket. Budget conservatively by using a bonus tax calculator before the payout. Consider setting aside part of your bonus for tax adjustments during year-end filing, as over-deduction in the bonus month may result in a refund but under-deduction may mean you owe LHDN when filing your return.
