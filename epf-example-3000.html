EPF guide

EPF Employee and Employer Contribution Malaysia

The Employees Provident Fund (EPF or KWSP) is Malaysia's mandatory retirement savings scheme. Both employees and employers are required by law to make monthly contributions. This page provides a detailed EPF breakdown example using a RM3,000 monthly salary, which is a common entry-level salary in Malaysia.

EPF Contribution Breakdown for RM3,000 Salary

For an employee below age 55 earning RM3,000 per month, the standard EPF contribution rates are:

ComponentRateMonthly Amount (RM)
Employee EPF Contribution11%330.00
Employer EPF Contribution12% or 13%360.00 or 390.00
Total Monthly EPF23% or 24%690.00 or 720.00

The employer rate of 12% or 13% depends on whether the employee's monthly wage falls below or above RM5,000. For a RM3,000 salary, the employer rate is 13% (RM390), giving a total monthly EPF contribution of RM720.

Step-by-Step: How RM3,000 Take-Home Is Affected

Here is how a RM3,000 gross salary is reduced to an estimated take-home amount, including EPF, SOCSO, EIS, and potential PCB:

DeductionCalculationAmount (RM)
Gross Monthly Salary—3,000.00
Employee EPF (11%)3,000 × 11%−330.00
SOCSO (0.5%)3,000 × 0.5%−15.00
EIS (0.2%)3,000 × 0.2%−6.00
PCB (estimated)May be RM0 for most cases−0.00
Estimated Take-Home Pay3,000 − 330 − 15 − 6≈ 2,649.00

For many employees earning RM3,000, the PCB deduction may be zero because the annual income after EPF relief and personal relief falls below the taxable threshold. However, this varies based on marital status, number of children, and other reliefs. Always check your actual pay slip for the exact figures.

EPF Account 1 vs Account 2

Your total EPF savings are divided between two accounts:

  • Account 1 (Akaun Persaraan — Retirement Account): Holds 70% of your total EPF balance. This account is strictly for retirement and can only be withdrawn at age 55 (full withdrawal), age 60 (full withdrawal of remaining balance), or under specific conditions such as total permanent disablement, leaving the country, or death. The funds in Account 1 are designed to provide long-term financial security after retirement.
  • Account 2 (Akaun Fleksibel — Flexible Account): Holds 30% of your total EPF balance. This account allows for partial withdrawals for approved purposes including housing loan down payments, home loan instalment payments, education (self or children), and medical expenses for critical illnesses. The flexible nature of Account 2 provides a safety net for significant life events.

For a RM3,000 salary with RM720 total monthly EPF, approximately RM504 goes to Account 1 and RM216 goes to Account 2 each month.

What Happens to EPF During Probation?

A common concern for new employees is whether EPF is deducted during the probation period. Under the EPF Act 1991, your employer is legally obligated to begin EPF contributions from your very first day of employment — there is no probation exemption. This applies regardless of whether you are on a probationary contract, temporary contract, or permanent contract.

If your employer does not deduct EPF during your probation period, they are violating the law. You can verify your contributions by checking your EPF statement online via the KWSP i-Akaun app or website. If contributions are missing, you can lodge a complaint with KWSP for investigation and recovery.

Common Mistakes for RM3,000 Salary Earners

  • Assuming no EPF during probation: EPF is mandatory from day one of employment, including during probation.
  • Not checking EPF statements: Always verify that your employer is contributing the correct amount by checking your KWSP i-Akaun regularly.
  • Confusing gross and net salary: When negotiating salary, remember that RM3,000 gross does not equal RM3,000 in hand. After EPF, SOCSO, and EIS, your take-home is approximately RM2,649 (assuming no PCB).
  • Not knowing about reduced contribution rates: Employees aged 55 and above are eligible for lower EPF contribution rates (5.5% for ages 55–60, 3.5% for ages 60+). Not being aware of this means missing out on higher take-home pay if eligible.
  • Ignoring the employer's contribution: Remember that your employer contributes 12% or 13% on top of your salary. This is free retirement savings and a significant employment benefit.
  • Forgetting about EPF dividend growth: EPF savings earn annual dividends. Historically, KWSP has declared dividends of 4% to 7% per year, meaning your retirement savings grow beyond just the contributions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much EPF is deducted from a RM3,000 salary in Malaysia?

For a RM3,000 monthly salary, the employee EPF deduction at 11% is RM330 per month. The employer contributes an additional 12% or 13% (RM360 or RM390 depending on the wage bracket), making the total EPF contribution between RM690 and RM720 per month. These rates apply to employees below age 55 under the standard statutory rates.

What is the difference between EPF Account 1 and Account 2?

As of the current EPF structure, your total EPF savings are split between two accounts. Account 1 (Akaun 1) holds 70% of your total EPF balance and is meant for retirement — you can only withdraw from it at age 55, 60, or under specific conditions like incapacitation. Account 2 (Akaun 2) holds 30% and allows partial withdrawals for housing, education, and health purposes. This restructuring was introduced to ensure sufficient retirement savings.

Will EPF still be deducted during my probation period?

Yes. Under Malaysian law, your employer must begin deducting EPF contributions from your first month of employment, including during probation. There is no legal exemption for probationary employees. If your employer fails to deduct EPF during probation, they are in violation of the EPF Act 1991 and you can lodge a complaint with KWSP.

Does a RM3,000 salary have PCB (income tax) deducted?

It depends on your circumstances. A RM3,000 monthly salary translates to RM36,000 annually. For an unmarried employee with no dependents, the taxable threshold is RM35,000 (after personal relief of RM9,000). With EPF relief of RM3,960 (RM330 × 12), your chargeable income would be approximately RM32,040, which falls below the taxable threshold. So most RM3,000 earners with no other income sources may not have PCB deducted. However, if you have additional taxable income or fewer reliefs, PCB may apply.

Can I opt for a lower EPF contribution rate?

Yes, but only under specific conditions. Employees aged 55 to 60 can elect to contribute at 5.5% (reduced from 11%). Employees aged 60 and above can elect to contribute at 3.5%. To opt for the reduced rate, you must submit the relevant EPF form (Borang A) to your employer. Choosing a lower rate means less retirement savings but higher take-home pay.

How does employer EPF contribution benefit me?

The employer's EPF contribution (12% or 13%) is paid by the employer on top of your salary — it is not deducted from your wages. This amount goes directly into your EPF account and grows with annual dividends declared by KWSP. Over a career, the employer's contribution can form a significant portion of your total retirement savings. For a RM3,000 salary, the employer contributes RM360 to RM390 monthly, which adds up to RM4,320 to RM4,680 per year.

Actual EPF contribution depends on official KWSP rules, employee age category, and current statutory rates. This page is for estimation purposes only and does not constitute financial advice.
