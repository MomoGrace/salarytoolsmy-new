Monthly Salary Calculator Malaysia

A monthly salary calculator helps you estimate how much of your salary remains after common statutory and voluntary deductions. In Malaysia, understanding the gap between your gross salary and take-home pay is essential for comparing job offers, planning household budgets and making sense of the figures on your payslip each month.

What Is Monthly Salary?

Monthly salary refers to the fixed amount of compensation an employee receives from their employer every month, typically credited on the last working day or a specific payroll date. In Malaysia, salary can be quoted in several ways:

  • Monthly gross salary – The total amount before any deductions, as stated in the employment contract or offer letter.
  • Monthly take-home pay (net salary) – The amount you actually receive after EPF, SOCSO, EIS, PCB and any other deductions are subtracted.
  • Annual salary – Monthly gross multiplied by 12 (plus any fixed bonuses or allowances).
  • Daily or hourly rate – Derived by dividing monthly salary by working days or hours.

The key distinction employees need to understand is that gross salary is not what lands in your bank account. Statutory deductions mandated by Malaysian law can reduce your take-home pay by 15% to 25% or more, depending on your salary level and tax bracket.

Monthly Gross vs Monthly Take-Home

When an employer offers you a monthly salary of RM5,000, that figure represents your gross pay. Before the money reaches your account, the following statutory deductions are typically applied:

  • EPF (Kumpulan Wang Simpanan Pekerja) – 11% employee contribution (default rate) deducted for your retirement savings. Employers add a matching 12% or 13% contribution on top.
  • SOCSO (PERKESO) – Social security contribution covering workplace injuries, invalidity and death benefits. The First Category (injury and invalidity) is the most common.
  • EIS (SIP) – Employment Insurance System contribution providing temporary financial support if you lose your job. The employee share is capped at wages of RM4,000.
  • PCB (LHDN Tax) – Monthly Tax Deduction (MTD) based on LHDN's tax schedules, spread across 12 months so you do not face a large lump-sum tax bill at year end.

How to Budget Based on Monthly Take-Home Pay

One of the most common financial mistakes in Malaysia is budgeting based on gross salary rather than net take-home pay. To build a realistic monthly budget:

  1. Start with your take-home figure – Use the net amount on your payslip, not the gross figure in your contract.
  2. Apply the 50/30/20 rule – Allocate roughly 50% to needs (rent, utilities, food, transport), 30% to wants (entertainment, dining) and 20% to savings or debt repayment.
  3. Account for variable expenses – Include annual commitments such as road tax renewal, insurance premiums and festive spending, divided across 12 months.
  4. Track bonuses separately – Do not rely on bonuses as part of your regular monthly income.

Converting Monthly Salary to Daily and Hourly Rates

In some situations, you may need to convert your monthly salary into a daily or hourly rate, such as when calculating overtime pay, comparing freelance offers or prorating salary for unpaid leave:

  • Daily rate = Monthly salary ÷ 26 working days. Example: RM5,000 ÷ 26 = RM192.31 per day.
  • Hourly rate = Daily rate ÷ 8 working hours. Example: RM192.31 ÷ 8 = RM24.04 per hour.

Some organisations use 22 working days per month (excluding weekends) which yields a higher daily rate. The formula you use should match the method stated in your employment contract or company HR policy.

Common Mistakes When Using Monthly Salary for Budgeting

  • Budgeting on gross pay – Planning expenses based on RM5,000 when your actual take-home is around RM4,200 leads to overspending.
  • Ignoring PCB adjustments – PCB amounts can change mid-year when you receive a bonus or when HR updates your tax profile.
  • Forgetting annual commitments – Road tax, car insurance and income tax filing are yearly expenses that should be spread across monthly budgets.
  • Assuming uniform deductions – Overtime pay, allowances and commissions can push you into a higher PCB bracket in certain months.
  • Not reviewing payslips – Errors in EPF or SOCSO registration, wrong tax categories or duplicate deductions can silently reduce your take-home pay.

Malaysia-Specific Notes

  • EPF contributions are mandatory for Malaysian citizens and permanent residents. Foreign workers contribute under a different schedule.
  • Employees aged 60 and above have reduced EPF contribution rates (5.5% employee, 6.5% employer for ages 60–75).
  • SOCSO coverage was extended to include foreign workers in certain sectors.
  • The PCB system uses MTD Schedule tables published by LHDN (Inland Revenue Board of Malaysia).
  • Zakat deductions are available for Muslim employees and can reduce PCB liability.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between monthly gross salary and monthly take-home pay in Malaysia?

Monthly gross salary is the total amount stated in your employment contract before any deductions. Take-home pay (net salary) is what you actually receive in your bank account after statutory deductions such as EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB have been subtracted. For example, a RM5,000 gross salary may result in a take-home pay of approximately RM4,020 to RM4,200 depending on your tax bracket and EPF election.

How are EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB deducted from my monthly salary?

These are statutory deductions required under Malaysian law. EPF (Employees Provident Fund) deducts 11% of your salary (or a lower elected rate) for retirement savings. SOCSO (Social Security Organisation) covers workplace injury and invalidity. EIS (Employment Insurance System) provides job-loss protection with contributions capped at a RM4,000 monthly wage ceiling for employee shares. PCB (Potongan Cukai Bulanan) is the monthly income tax deduction based on LHDN schedules.

Why does my take-home pay differ from the gross salary stated in my employment letter?

Your employment letter states the gross (before deductions) salary. Malaysian employers are legally required to deduct EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB from each payroll cycle. Additional deductions may include zakat (for Muslim employees), salary advances, loan repayments or private insurance premiums. The cumulative effect of these deductions typically reduces your gross salary by 15% to 25%.

How do I convert my monthly salary to a daily or hourly rate in Malaysia?

A common method is to divide your monthly salary by 26 working days to get the daily rate, then divide by 8 working hours to get the hourly rate. For example, RM5,000 ÷ 26 = RM192.31 per day, and RM192.31 ÷ 8 = RM24.04 per hour. Some organisations use 22 working days (excluding weekends) which gives a different figure. Always check your employment contract or company policy for the exact method used.

What common mistakes do people make when budgeting based on monthly salary?

The most common mistake is budgeting based on gross salary instead of take-home pay. Other errors include forgetting variable deductions such as annual bonus tax (PCB), not accounting for EPF voluntary contributions, overlooking employer allowances (travel, parking) that may be taxable, and assuming the same take-home amount every month when overtime or allowances cause fluctuations.

Is there a legal minimum monthly salary in Malaysia?

Yes. Under Malaysia's Employment Act 1955, the minimum wage is periodically revised by the government. For Peninsular Malaysia, the minimum monthly wage was set at RM1,500 per month (effective from 2023 onwards). Sabah, Sarawak and Labuan may have different minimum wage rates. Employers must comply with the applicable rate based on the employee's location and employment category.
