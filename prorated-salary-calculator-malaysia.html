Partial month salary guide

Prorated Salary Calculator Malaysia – Calculate Partial Month Salary

Prorated salary means paying only for the eligible paid days in a month instead of the full monthly salary. In Malaysia, this is common for new joiners who start after the first day of the month, employees who resign before month-end, unpaid leave deductions, and mid-month salary revisions. Understanding prorating helps both employees and payroll teams prevent disputes and explain payslip differences clearly.

What prorated salary means in Malaysia

When payroll is prorated, the employee is paid for time actually served or eligible paid days within that salary cycle. The key point is that the company must apply its chosen method consistently based on employment contract terms and payroll policy. Confusion usually happens when one person assumes calendar-day prorating while payroll applies working-day prorating.

Prorated pay affects not just gross monthly pay but potentially statutory deductions and PCB too, since taxable income can vary in that month. That is why employees often see a noticeably different first or final payslip compared with normal full-month salary.

Common prorated salary situations

1) New joiner

You start after the first day of the month, so salary is paid from joining date to month-end according to payroll method.

2) Resignation or final month

You leave before month-end, so final salary is calculated up to your last paid day, then adjusted for leave balance and other final-pay items.

3) Unpaid leave (No Pay Leave)

Unpaid leave days are deducted from monthly salary using the company prorating formula. This can reduce net salary more than expected if multiple leave days fall in one cycle.

4) Mid-month salary adjustment

If a salary increment or salary revision is effective during the month, payroll may split the month into old-rate days and new-rate days.

Prorated salary formula

Prorated salary = Monthly salary ÷ total salary days × eligible paid days

Where:

  • Monthly salary = normal full-month gross salary
  • Total salary days = either calendar days in month or working days, depending on payroll policy
  • Eligible paid days = days payable for that cycle after join/leave/unpaid-leave adjustments

Working days vs calendar days: why results differ

Some payroll policies prorate using calendar days in the month (28, 29, 30 or 31). Others use total working days for that specific month. The same salary can produce different outputs under the two methods. This is usually a policy choice defined by company practice and employment terms.

  • Calendar-day method: easier to apply consistently across many staff categories.
  • Working-day method: can align closer to attendance-based operations but changes month to month.

If your computed amount differs from payroll, first check method choice before assuming an error.

Employee perspective

  • Ask HR which prorating method is used before signing an offer.
  • For first-month salary, check joining date counted by payroll cut-off.
  • For resignation month, check last paid day and any unpaid leave offsets.
  • Use net-pay calculators after prorating to estimate cashflow for commitments like rent and instalments.

Employer payroll perspective

Consistent prorating reduces disputes and exit-month complaints. HR and payroll teams should document formula, denominator logic, and examples in onboarding or policy guides. For final salary, verify notice-period treatment, annual leave balance, deductions and statutory files before release. Clear payslip remarks save significant back-and-forth with employees.

Common mistakes with prorated salary

  • Mixing calendar-day and working-day formula in different months without policy basis.
  • Using full-month salary in final pay despite partial service month.
  • Ignoring payroll cut-off timing, causing expected days to shift to next cycle.
  • Not reflecting unpaid leave deductions clearly on payslip line items.
  • Assuming prorated gross and prorated net move by exactly the same amount.

Step-by-step way to calculate prorated salary manually

Step 1: confirm monthly gross salary used by payroll (base salary only or base plus fixed allowance). Step 2: confirm denominator method for that payroll cycle: calendar days in month or working days. Step 3: count eligible paid days based on join date, resignation date and unpaid leave records. Step 4: apply formula and round using payroll policy. Step 5: check if additional items such as claim reimbursements, overtime, salary advances or final leave encashment are processed in the same cycle, because those can make net pay differ from your prorated gross estimate.

Employees often make the mistake of counting weekends as paid days under a working-day method, or excluding public holidays under a calendar-day method. Always align your counting method with payroll policy first. A correct method with wrong day count still produces the wrong result.

Final salary month: items beyond prorated base pay

In resignation months, final payroll can include more than prorated base salary: unpaid leave deductions, replacement leave treatment, annual leave encashment, notice-related adjustments, bonus eligibility rules and company asset recovery. Because several items move at once, employees may think the prorated formula is wrong when the real difference comes from final settlement components outside base pay.

From an employer perspective, documenting each final-pay line item and sharing a concise breakdown helps reduce disputes. A simple one-page final-pay summary with formula, day count and adjustments usually prevents repeated clarification emails.

Prorated Salary Calculator Malaysia FAQ

1. Is prorated salary legal in Malaysia?

Prorating is a common payroll practice for partial-month service and unpaid leave, subject to employment terms and payroll policy.

2. Which method is correct: working days or calendar days?

Both are used in practice; the key is consistent policy application and transparent communication to employees.

3. Why is first-month salary lower than expected?

Usually because you were paid for eligible days after joining date, not a full month.

4. Does prorated salary affect EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB?

Yes, because those calculations depend on payroll income for that month and statutory treatment.

5. Can unpaid leave be deducted using prorated formula?

Yes. Many payroll teams convert unpaid leave days into deduction values using the same denominator logic.

6. Is final month salary always delayed?

Not always, but final payroll may take longer due to leave reconciliation, claims and compliance checks.

7. Should I use gross or net prorated estimate for budgeting?

Use net estimate for spending plans, but keep gross estimate to cross-check payslip calculations.

8. What if my payslip does not explain the calculation?

Request the exact formula, denominator and eligible paid day count from payroll or HR in writing.
