Take Home Pay Malaysia: Salary After EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB

Take-home pay is the amount you may receive after payroll deductions. In Malaysia, common deductions include EPF, SOCSO, EIS and monthly PCB tax where applicable.

What is take-home pay?

Take-home pay, also called net salary or net pay, is the final amount credited to your bank account after all mandatory and optional deductions are subtracted from your gross salary. Your offer letter typically states the gross figure, but the number that actually lands in your bank each month is always lower because of statutory payroll deductions. Understanding the gap between gross and net is one of the most practical salary skills for any employee in Malaysia, especially when comparing job offers or planning monthly budgets.

What affects take-home pay?

Several factors determine how much of your gross salary becomes take-home pay. The main items are:

  • Gross monthly salary — the starting amount before any deductions.
  • Employee EPF contribution — typically 11% of wages (or a reduced rate for employees below age 55 under certain categories).
  • SOCSO contribution — calculated based on the employee's wage category and PERKESO schedules.
  • EIS contribution — 0.2% of wages up to the RM6,000 ceiling.
  • PCB monthly tax deduction — estimated by the employer based on LHDN's tax schedules and employee tax profile.
  • Other adjustments — bonus, overtime, allowances, unpaid leave, or company-specific deductions (loan repayments, insurance, etc.).

Because multiple items are subtracted, the cumulative effect can be significant. A RM5,000 gross salary could result in a take-home pay of roughly RM3,900 to RM4,200 depending on individual PCB tax bracket and specific payroll configuration.

Take-home pay formula (simplified estimate)

The basic logic for estimating take-home pay in Malaysia is:

Take-Home Pay = Gross Salary - Employee EPF - SOCSO - EIS - PCB

Each deduction is calculated independently. EPF is a percentage of wages, SOCSO and EIS follow PERKESO schedules, and PCB is derived from LHDN tax tables based on your monthly taxable income and declared tax status. While this formula gives a useful planning estimate, actual payslip amounts may differ due to statutory rounding, specific wage definitions, and company payroll settings.

Why take-home pay can vary month to month

Even with a fixed base salary, your monthly bank-in amount may fluctuate. Common reasons include:

  • Overtime payments — additional income that may be subject to EPF and PCB adjustments.
  • Bonus or commission — one-time or variable payments that can push a month's income into a higher PCB bracket.
  • Unpaid leave — reduces contributable wages, which lowers EPF, SOCSO, EIS and potentially PCB for that month.
  • PCB schedule changes — employers may update PCB calculations mid-year based on revised LHDN directives or employee tax profile updates.
  • Mid-year salary increment — a raise in gross salary changes all percentage-based deductions proportionally.

Tracking these variations over time helps you understand your real monthly disposable income and plan budgets more accurately.

Common mistakes when estimating take-home pay

  • Using gross salary as budget base: Budgeting should be based on take-home pay, not the offer letter figure. The difference can be 15% to 30% depending on income level and deductions.
  • Forgetting that employer EPF does not affect net pay: Only employee EPF is deducted from wages. Employer EPF is paid separately and should not be subtracted when estimating your take-home amount.
  • Assuming PCB is the final tax: PCB is a monthly advance payment. Your actual tax liability is determined when you file your annual income tax return. You may receive a refund or owe additional tax.
  • Ignoring allowance variability: Travel, housing, and meal allowances may or may not be subject to EPF and PCB depending on company policy and tax treatment. Check with your HR team.
  • Not accounting for prorated months: If you join or leave a company mid-month, all deductions are prorated based on actual days worked, resulting in a different take-home amount than a full month.

Frequently asked questions

What is take-home pay in Malaysia?

Take-home pay (also called net salary) is the amount credited to your bank account after all statutory and other deductions are subtracted from your gross salary. In Malaysia, the main deductions are EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB tax. Take-home pay is always lower than gross salary, sometimes significantly so depending on your income level and deduction category.

How is take-home pay calculated in Malaysia?

Take-home pay is calculated by subtracting EPF employee contribution, SOCSO contribution, EIS contribution and PCB (monthly tax deduction) from your gross monthly salary. The formula is: Take-Home Pay = Gross Salary - Employee EPF - SOCSO - EIS - PCB. Some employees may also have additional deductions for overtime adjustments, allowance recovery or company-specific items.

Why is my take-home pay lower than my offer letter salary?

Offer letters typically state the gross (before-deduction) salary. After mandatory deductions for EPF, SOCSO, EIS and PCB, the actual amount credited to your bank is lower. For example, a RM5,000 gross salary could result in roughly RM3,900 to RM4,200 take-home pay depending on your specific deduction rates and tax bracket. This is normal and expected in Malaysian payroll.

Does employer EPF affect my take-home pay?

No. Employer EPF is paid by the employer on top of your salary cost. It does not reduce your take-home pay. Only the employee EPF portion (typically 11% of wages) is deducted from your gross salary before the bank-in amount is calculated.

Is PCB the same as income tax?

PCB (Potongan Cukai Bulanan) is the monthly tax deduction withheld by your employer based on LHDN schedules. It is an advance payment towards your annual income tax liability. When you file your annual tax return, the PCB paid throughout the year is offset against your total tax. You may receive a refund or need to pay the difference depending on your actual tax bill.

Can take-home pay change from month to month?

Yes. Take-home pay can vary due to overtime payments, bonus or commission, unpaid leave, allowance changes, PCB schedule adjustments, or mid-year salary increments. Even if your base salary stays the same, any variable income component or leave adjustment can shift the final bank-in amount.
